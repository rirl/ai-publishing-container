== Part I: Logical Reconstruction

The argument must be stated with maximum precision before any critique proceeds.

=== The Inferential Skeleton

*P1.* Certain experiences of beauty produce a distinctive longing (_Sehnsucht_) — not satisfaction, but an inconsolable ache.

*P2.* No finite object satisfies this longing. Beautiful things _awaken_ the desire but cannot _fulfill_ it.

*P3.* Every natural desire has a corresponding real object capable of satisfying it. (The _Desire-Object Principle._)

*P4.* Therefore, the object capable of satisfying _Sehnsucht_ exists — and is not finite.

*C.* Therefore, _Sehnsucht_ points toward a transcendent reality — identified by Lewis as God, or participation in divine Beauty.

=== Three Distinct Inferential Moves

These must never be conflated.

*Move 1: From personal phenomenological datum to universal human desire.* +
Lewis treats his own _Sehnsucht_ as structurally representative. This universalization is asserted, not argued.

*Move 2: From unsatisfied desire to real corresponding object.* +
The load-bearing premise. Borrowed implicitly from Aquinas. Deeply vulnerable.

*Move 3: From transcendent object to the Christian God.* +
Massively underdetermined even if Moves 1 and 2 succeed. A Platonic Form, Schopenhauerian Will, or Kantian noumenon could occupy this logical slot equally.

'''

== Part II: Internal Critique

=== Problem 1: The Desire-Object Principle Is False as Stated

Lewis writes in _Weight of Glory_: a man's hunger does not prove he will get food, but it proves he is the kind of creature whose food exists.

This analogical principle fails under scrutiny:

* The desire for immortality exists universally. It does not follow that immortality obtains.
* The desire for perfect justice exists. No corresponding worldly object satisfies it.
* The desire to fly unaided exists. No natural object fulfills it.

The principle holds only in a weak reformulation: _some_ natural desires have _some_ real corresponding objects. This weak version *cannot carry the inferential weight Lewis requires.*

=== Problem 2: The Phenomenological Datum Is Already Interpreted

Lewis does not neutrally describe _Sehnsucht_. He describes it as _longing for an unknown transcendent X_. The transcendent orientation is built into the description from the outset.

A neutral phenomenological description would yield only:

* An experience of _aesthetic excess_ — the object provokes a response disproportionate to its finite character.
* A felt _inadequacy_ of the finite object relative to the desire it awakens.
* A structure of _irreducible tension_ within the experience itself.

None of these neutral descriptions entail transcendence. Lewis interprets the datum before examining it.

=== Problem 3: The Move from Transcendence to Theism Is a Non Sequitur

Even granting P1–P4 in full, the conclusion is only: _a transcendent object of beauty exists._ This is not equivalent to:

* A personal God exists.
* The Christian God exists.
* Divine Beauty is participable by human persons.

The slide between these is rhetorical in _Weight of Glory_ and structural in _Mere Christianity_. Neither text argues the transition explicitly.

== Part III: Husserl — Full Treatment

=== Logical Investigations: Intentionality Without Transcendental Reduction

In the _Logical Investigations_, Husserl establishes intentionality as the fundamental structure of consciousness: every act of consciousness is consciousness _of_ something. The intentional act has:

* A *quality* — the type of act: perceiving, judging, wishing.
* A *matter* — what the act is directed toward, and how it is directed.
* A *representative content* — what fills the act intuitively.

For Lewis's argument this matters immediately: _Sehnsucht_ is an intentional act. It is directed _toward_ something. The question is whether that directedness constitutes evidence for the existence of its object.

Husserl's answer at this stage is careful: intentionality does not guarantee object-existence. An act can be directed toward a non-existent object (_golden mountain_, _round square_). The mere structure of longing-toward does not establish that what is longed for exists.

*Lewis's P3 is already undermined at the most basic level of intentionality theory.*

=== Ideen I: Transcendental Idealism and the Reduction

In _Ideen I_, Husserl introduces the _epoché_ — the phenomenological reduction that brackets the natural attitude's assumption of a mind-independent world. What remains is pure consciousness and its _noema_ — the object as intended.

Two implications for Lewis:

*First:* The transcendental reduction reveals that the world's meaning is _constituted_ by consciousness. The "beyond" that Lewis senses in aesthetic experience is itself a _noematic_ structure — the object _as intended_ by consciousness, not a mind-independent transcendent realm.

*Second:* The _epoché_ applied to Lewis's own argument would require bracketing the natural-attitude assumption that _Sehnsucht_ points toward a really-existing transcendent God. What remains after the reduction is: a consciousness directed toward an inexhaustible noematic pole. This is philosophically significant — but it is not evidence for theism.

=== Ideen II: The Lived Body and Aesthetic Constitution

_Ideen II_ introduces the crucial distinction Lewis never makes:

* *_Leib_* — the lived body, experienced from within, the zero-point of intentional orientation.
* *_Körper_* — the physical body, the body as object among objects, measurable and extended.

Aesthetic experience, for Husserl in _Ideen II_, is not purely cognitive. The _Leib_ is the organ of perception — it is through bodily orientation, kinaesthesia, and motor response that the aesthetic object is constituted as having depth, weight, resonance.

*Lewis's account of _Sehnsucht_ is almost entirely intellectualist.* He does not account for its bodily constitution — the way the _Leib_ is already implicated in the aesthetic response before any cognitive interpretation occurs.

This gap is not trivial. If aesthetic experience is constituted through _Leib_, then the "beyond" that Lewis senses may be the _depth of bodily intentionality itself_ — not a pointer toward a supernatural realm.

=== Crisis: The Lifeworld and Sedimented Meaning

In the _Crisis_, Husserl introduces the _Lebenswelt_ — the pre-theoretical, sedimented world of lived experience that underlies all abstraction.

Applied to Lewis: _Sehnsucht_ does not arrive in a neutral phenomenological field. It arrives in a _Lebenswelt_ already structured by:

* Christian theological categories.
* Romantic literary tradition (Wordsworth, MacDonald, Novalis).
* A specifically Northern European aesthetic sensibility.

Lewis's phenomenological datum is _sedimented_ — it carries the weight of a particular _Lebenswelt_. His argument presents this culturally-specific experience as a universal human datum. The _Crisis_ framework exposes this move as methodologically unjustified.

== Part IV: Edith Stein — Full Treatment

Stein is the most philosophically complex interlocutor here. She cannot simply be mobilized against Lewis. Her work partially vindicates him — while simultaneously exposing where his argument is philosophically thin.

=== On the Problem of Empathy (1917): _Einfühlung_ and Aesthetic Intersubjectivity

Stein's doctoral dissertation establishes _Einfühlung_ (empathy) as a _sui generis_ intentional act — irreducible to inference, projection, or analogy.

In _Einfühlung_:

* I apprehend the Other's experience _as theirs_ — not as a copy of my own.
* The Other's experiential life is given _non-primordially_ — I do not live through it as I live through my own, but I genuinely apprehend it.
* This is a *genuine cognitive act* with its own intentional structure.

The relevance to Lewis is direct and underappreciated. Lewis's _Sehnsucht_ is described as a _solitary_ experience. But Stein's _Einfühlung_ account shows that aesthetic experience is *intersubjectively constituted*.

Two implications follow:

*First:* The "excess" Lewis identifies in aesthetic experience may be the _depth of intersubjective constitution_ — the way the aesthetic object carries the sedimented responses of countless other subjects — rather than a pointer toward divine transcendence.

*Second:* Through _Einfühlung_, the structure of aesthetic longing is apprehended as intersubjectively shared. Stein provides what Lewis lacks — a rigorous account of _why_ aesthetic longing might be structurally universal.

=== Finite and Eternal Being (1950): Phenomenology and Thomistic Metaphysics

This is Stein's most ambitious work — an explicit attempt to synthesize Husserlian phenomenology with Thomistic ontology.

Stein's central move: *Being itself (_Sein_) is experienced in the act of consciousness.* The _I am_ of self-consciousness is a participation in Being as such. And Being as such, following Aquinas, is identical with God (_esse ipsum subsistens_).

The contrast with Lewis is philosophically decisive:

* *Lewis's structure:* Phenomenological datum → inference → transcendent God as _conclusion_.
* *Stein's structure:* Phenomenological datum → transcendental analysis → transcendent Being as _condition of possibility_ of the datum.

Transcendence is not reached by inference. It is _already operative_ within the experience. This is closer to Bonaventure's _itinerarium_ than to analogical inference — and it is a stronger, more rigorous position than Lewis deploys.

=== Ways to Know God: Limiting the Claim Honestly

In this late essay, Stein distinguishes three ways of knowing God:

* *Rational demonstration* — the Thomistic five ways.
* *Faith* — supernatural assent.
* *Mystical experience* — direct experiential disclosure.

Stein argues that phenomenological analysis belongs to the third category — but only when the reduction is _completed_ rather than arrested. Lewis's argument arrests the reduction at the level of _Sehnsucht_ and draws a theistic inference. Stein would argue this is methodologically premature.

The full phenomenological reduction does not yield theism as a logical conclusion. It yields an openness — a _Offenheit_ — toward Being that is compatible with theism but does not compel it.

=== Stein's Summary Position on Lewis

Stein *partially vindicates* Lewis on three points:

* The universalizability of aesthetic longing via _Einfühlung_.
* The legitimacy of treating phenomenological experience as philosophically informative about transcendence.
* The participatory ontology that underlies the experience of beauty.

Stein *undermines* Lewis on two points:

* The inferential structure is too crude — transcendence is a condition of experience, not its conclusion.
* The move from transcendence to the Christian God requires the resources of faith, not phenomenology alone.

== Part V: Sartre — Full Treatment

Sartre's engagement is tripartite. Each text delivers a distinct and compounding critique.

=== L'Imaginaire (1940): The Phenomenology of the Aesthetic Image

This is the most directly relevant Sartrean text and the most ignored in Lewis scholarship.

Sartre's central thesis: *the aesthetic object is an imaginary object, not a real one.* When I perceive a painting, I perceive an _imaginary_ object that the canvas and pigment occasion. The aesthetic experience requires a *neutralization of the real*.

The structure is precise:

* The real object (canvas, pigment) functions as an *analogon* — a material support for the imaginary object.
* The imaginary object is constituted by consciousness through an act of *imageant consciousness* (_conscience imageante_).
* The aesthetic experience is therefore an experience of *nothingness* — consciousness holding the real at distance in order to constitute the imaginary.

Applied to Lewis's _Sehnsucht_: the "beyond" that Lewis experiences in aesthetic beauty is not a pointer toward transcendence. It is the structure of imageant consciousness itself. The inexhaustibility of the aesthetic object — its seeming to point beyond itself — is the inexhaustibility of the imaginary, constituted by consciousness and not received from a transcendent source.

This is a technically grounded alternative to Lewis's interpretation. It explains the same phenomenological datum without any appeal to transcendence.

=== Esquisse d'une théorie des émotions (1939): Emotion as Magical Transformation

In the _Esquisse_, Sartre argues that emotion is a *magical transformation of the world* — a way consciousness reconfigures its relationship to a situation it cannot master through instrumental action.

Applied to _Sehnsucht_: Lewis's longing is consciousness _magically transforming_ the aesthetic situation — reframing finite beauty as a pointer toward the infinite — in order to manage the existential weight of the experience.

*This is not evidence for transcendence.* It is evidence for a specific emotional-intentional strategy consciousness adopts when confronted with aesthetic excess. The intentional content (pointing toward God) is a magical reconfiguration of the world, not a reliable disclosure of it.

=== L'Être et le Néant (1943): Consciousness, Bad Faith, and the Desire for Being

==== The Pour-soi and the En-soi

For Sartre, consciousness (_pour-soi_, being-for-itself) is:

* Radically *non-substantial* — it has no fixed nature.
* Constitutively defined by *_néantisation_* — the nihilating power that separates it from the world.
* *Temporally ecstatic* — always ahead of itself, never coincident with itself.

The fundamental desire of consciousness is to become _pour-soi-en-soi_ — the impossible synthesis of freedom and fixed being. This is *the desire to be God* (_le désir d'être Dieu_).

Applied to Lewis: the desire for God is not evidence that God exists. It is evidence that consciousness is constitutively defined by a *lack that cannot be filled* — not by any finite object and not by any infinite one either.

==== The Critique of Bad Faith

Bad faith (_mauvaise foi_) is the self-deceptive denial of radical freedom through appeal to:

* A fixed human nature (_esprit de sérieux_).
* An external determinism.
* A transcendent _telos_ that defines one's being in advance.

Lewis's argument exhibits precisely the structure of _esprit de sérieux_: it treats _Sehnsucht_ as evidence of a fixed human nature oriented toward God, thereby converting radical existential openness into metaphysical destiny. For Sartre, this is not merely a philosophical disagreement — it is an *ethical failure*.

==== The Look and Intersubjectivity

Lewis's account of _Sehnsucht_ is constitutively solitary. Sartre's account of intersubjectivity — structured by the _Regard_ (the Look) — shows that subjectivity is always already implicated in a field of mutual objectification. The beautiful object is never encountered in a purely private phenomenological field. Lewis's privatization of _Sehnsucht_ ignores this constitutive intersubjective dimension.

=== Sartre's Summary Position on Lewis

Sartre delivers four compounding critiques:

* *_L'Imaginaire_:* The "beyond" of aesthetic experience is the structure of imageant consciousness, requiring no transcendence.
* *_Esquisse_:* _Sehnsucht_ is an emotional-magical transformation of the world, not a disclosure of it.
* *_L'Être et le Néant_:* The desire for God is evidence of constitutive lack, not of God's existence.
* *The bad faith critique:* Lewis's argument structurally denies radical freedom by converting existential openness into metaphysical destiny.

'''

== Part VI: Comparative Synthesis

=== The Same Datum, Four Interpretations

All four thinkers engage the same phenomenological datum Lewis identifies — the sense of excess, inexhaustibility, and beyond-pointing in aesthetic experience. Their interpretations are mutually exclusive.

[cols="1,3", options="header"]
|===
|Thinker
|Account of Aesthetic Excess

|*Lewis*
|Sign pointing toward transcendent divine Beauty

|*Husserl*
|Horizon-structure of intentionality; noematic inexhaustibility

|*Stein*
|Participation in Being; transcendence as condition, not conclusion

|*Sartre*
|Structure of imageant consciousness; emotional-magical transformation

|*Merleau-Ponty*
|Chiasmic reversibility of _chair_; world's own depth

|*Heidegger*
|Disclosure of _Sein_ through beings; world's self-showing
|===

Lewis's interpretation is *one* of these. It is not uniquely supported by the datum.

=== The Intersubjectivity Gap

Every thinker examined — Husserl (_Ideen II_, _Crisis_), Stein (_Einfühlung_), Sartre (_Regard_), Merleau-Ponty (_chair_) — demonstrates that aesthetic experience is *intersubjectively constituted*. Lewis's account is structurally solitary. This is not a minor oversight. It means Lewis's phenomenological starting point is already a distortion of the actual structure of aesthetic experience.

=== The Stein Problem

Stein represents the most philosophically serious challenge to a purely negative assessment — because she operates within a theistic framework and still finds Lewis's argument insufficient. Her position can be stated precisely:

* Lewis is right that phenomenological experience is philosophically informative about transcendence.
* Lewis is wrong that the inferential structure he uses is adequate.
* The correct account requires: _Einfühlung_ for universalizability, participatory ontology for the transcendence claim, and faith for the specifically theistic conclusion.

*Stein raises the bar for Lewis's argument rather than simply rejecting it.*

== Part VII: The Husserlian-Steinian Reconstruction

Having diagnosed Lewis's deficiencies and surveyed the tradition, the argument can now be rebuilt on rigorous foundations.

=== Exact Diagnosis of Lewis's Deficiencies

==== Deficiency 1: The Desire-Object Principle Is Asserted, Not Grounded

Lewis's P3 floats free of any account of _why_ it should be true. It is borrowed from Aquinas without the metaphysical infrastructure Aquinas requires to support it.

Aquinas's version works within a teleological ontology where:

* Nature is constituted by final causality.
* Every potency is ordered toward an act.
* The ordering is guaranteed by divine creation.

Without this infrastructure, P3 is a bare assertion. Lewis uses the conclusion (theistic ontology) to support a premise that is supposed to generate the conclusion. *This is the circularity at the argument's core.*

==== Deficiency 2: _Sehnsucht_ Is Described Pre-Critically

Lewis does not apply any form of reduction to isolate the datum. He does not account for bodily constitution (_Leib_), address intersubjective sedimentation, or distinguish between the _act_ of longing and its _noematic content_.

==== Deficiency 3: The Universalization Move Is Unsupported

Lewis moves from _his_ experience of _Sehnsucht_ to a _universal human structure_ without argument. The universalization of a first-person datum requires a rigorous account of intersubjective constitution — which Lewis entirely lacks.

==== Deficiency 4: Transcendence Is Inferred Rather Than Phenomenologically Disclosed

Lewis treats transcendence as the _conclusion_ of an inference. This makes the theistic claim maximally vulnerable to alternative explanations. What is required is an account where transcendence is shown to be _operative within_ the datum — a condition of its possibility rather than its conclusion.

=== The Husserlian Reconstruction

==== Step 1: Replacing P3 with Horizon-Structure

The Desire-Object Principle must be entirely replaced. Its Husserlian substitute is the *theory of intentional fulfillment and horizon.*

* Every intentional act is directed toward an object with a specific *fulfillment condition*.
* Every act is surrounded by a *horizon* of co-intended but unfulfilled meaning — an open field of further possible determinations.
* The horizon is *genuinely intentional* — not a mere psychological fringe but a structured anticipation built into the act itself.

*P3 (Husserlian):* The intentional structure of _Sehnsucht_ includes a horizon of fulfillment that is not closed by any finite object. This horizon is not empty — it has a specific _noematic_ character: it anticipates an inexhaustible fullness of Being that finite objects gesture toward but cannot provide.

This is philosophically stronger than Lewis's original P3 because:

* It does not assert a metaphysical correspondence between desire and reality.
* It describes a _structural feature of the act itself_ — phenomenologically demonstrable.
* It does not presuppose theism to establish the claim.

==== Step 2: Applying the Reduction to _Sehnsucht_

The _epoché_ must be applied to Lewis's datum before any inference proceeds. This means bracketing:

* The natural-attitude assumption that _Sehnsucht_ points toward a really-existing God.
* The culturally sedimented Christian framework Lewis brings to the experience.
* The autobiographical narrative of _Surprised by Joy_ that shapes the description.

*The Reduced Datum:* A structure of consciousness in which a finite aesthetic object is given as _pointing beyond itself_ toward an inexhaustible noematic pole — a pole that is genuinely intended but never fully present, never adequately fulfilled by any finite givenness.

This reduced datum is philosophically neutral at the level of natural theology. It establishes: *the intentional structure of aesthetic experience includes a constitutive orientation toward an inexhaustible fullness that transcends every finite instantiation.*

==== Step 3: Ideen II — Grounding _Sehnsucht_ in the Lived Body

_Ideen II_ requires that this structure be grounded in the _Leib_:

* _Sehnsucht_ is constituted through kinesthetic resonance, postural attunement, and motor response to the aesthetic object.
* The _inexhaustibility_ of the aesthetic object is first _felt_ in the body's inability to adequately fulfill its resonance — not first apprehended cognitively.
* The _Leib_ itself is the site of the original tension between finite presence and inexhaustible orientation.

This strengthens the reconstructed argument against Sartre's _L'Imaginaire_ critique. Sartre's imageant consciousness involves the neutralization of the real — a primarily cognitive act. But bodily resonance is not neutralized in aesthetic experience. The body's kinesthetic response cannot be reduced to imageant consciousness without remainder.

==== Step 4: Crisis — Addressing the Sedimentation Problem

Even if _Sehnsucht_ is sedimented by a particular _Lebenswelt_, genetic phenomenological analysis can ask whether this sedimentation is *adventitious* (culturally contingent) or *founded* (grounded in structures of consciousness that precede any particular cultural formation).

The Husserlian argument: the horizon-structure of intentionality, the inexhaustibility of the noematic pole, and the bodily constitution of aesthetic experience are not features of one _Lebenswelt_. They are *transcendental structures* that any _Lebenswelt_ presupposes. The Christian-Romantic sedimentation shapes _how_ Lewis articulates the experience — it does not create the underlying structure.

*This is a partial defense of Lewis's universalization move — on rigorous rather than rhetorical grounds.*

=== The Steinian Reconstruction

==== Step 1: _Einfühlung_ — Grounding the Universalization

In aesthetic experience, I apprehend the object _as_ beautiful — within an intersubjective field of co-constitution. Through _Einfühlung_, I apprehend _other subjects' experiences_ of the same object — not by inference, but by a direct, non-primordial intentional act.

The beautiful object carries within its _noema_ the sedimented aesthetic responses of other subjects — it is given as _inexhaustible_ precisely because it exceeds any single subject's apprehension.

*The reconstructed universalization claim:* _Sehnsucht_ is not merely a private longing. Through _Einfühlung_, the structure of aesthetic longing is apprehended as intersubjectively shared — a feature of the aesthetic object's _noema_ accessible to any perceiving subject.

This is the rigorous version of Lewis's Move 1. It does not merely assert universality — it grounds it in the intersubjective constitution of aesthetic experience.

==== Step 2: Finite and Eternal Being — Transcendence as Condition, Not Conclusion

Stein's argument in _Finite and Eternal Being_:

* The _I am_ of self-consciousness is a participation in Being (_Sein_) as such.
* Being as such is not constituted by consciousness — consciousness _participates_ in it.
* The inexhaustible fullness that the aesthetic object's noematic horizon gestures toward is the _presence of Being itself_ — operative within the experience as its ground, not inferred from it as its conclusion.

*The reconstructed transcendence claim:* The inexhaustibility of the noematic horizon in aesthetic experience is the _operative presence of Being_ — an inexhaustible fullness that grounds finite beings without being reducible to them. Transcendence is not the conclusion of the argument. It is the *condition under which the argument's datum is possible.*

This is a *transcendental argument* in the strict sense.

==== Step 3: Ways to Know God — Limiting the Claim Honestly

The phenomenological analysis establishes: an inexhaustible fullness of Being is operatively present in aesthetic experience as its condition of possibility.

It does not establish: this Being is personal, this Being is the Christian God, this Being is participable through worship.

The move to specifically theistic conclusions requires the resources of faith — not as an irrational supplement but as a _different mode of intentional access_ to the same Being that phenomenology discloses obliquely.

=== The Reconstructed Argument — Formal Statement

*P1 (Husserlian-Steinian):* Aesthetic experience, when rigorously examined after phenomenological reduction, reveals a constitutive intentional structure: the beautiful object is given as inexhaustibly pointing beyond itself toward a noematic pole of fullness that no finite object can adequately fulfill.

*P2:* This structure is not a contingent feature of one subject's psychology. Through _Einfühlung_, it is disclosed as intersubjectively constituted — a feature of aesthetic experience as such, accessible to any perceiving subject.

*P3:* The structure is not merely formal. The inexhaustible noematic pole is not empty anticipation. Transcendental analysis shows that it is the operative presence of Being itself — an inexhaustible fullness that grounds finite beings as their condition of possibility.

*P4:* This operative presence of Being is not inferred _from_ the datum as an external conclusion. It is disclosed _within_ the datum as its transcendental ground.

*C1:* Aesthetic experience discloses the operative presence of an inexhaustible fullness of Being that transcends every finite instantiation.

*C2 (Honest Limit):* Whether this Being is the Christian God is a question phenomenology opens but cannot answer by its own resources alone.

'''

== Part VIII: Stress-Testing the Reconstruction Against Sartre

The reconstructed argument now faces Sartre's three critiques. Each is engaged precisely.

=== Test 1: Against L'Imaginaire

*Sartre's critique:* The "beyond" of aesthetic experience is the structure of imageant consciousness — the neutralization of the real in favor of the imaginary. No transcendence required.

*The reconstructed argument's response:*

*First:* Sartre's account of imageant consciousness presupposes the intentional structure it is supposed to explain. The _analogon_ (canvas, pigment) must be _apprehended as pointing beyond itself_ toward the imaginary object. But this structure of _pointing-beyond_ is precisely what the reconstructed argument identifies as primary. Sartre has not explained it — he has *relocated* it.

*Second:* The _Leib_-constitution of _Sehnsucht_ resists reduction to imageant consciousness. Sartre's account involves the *neutralization of the real* — a cognitive distancing. But bodily resonance with the aesthetic object is not neutralized in aesthetic experience. The _Leib_ remains engaged. Imageant consciousness does not account for this.

*Third:* Sartre's account still requires explaining _why_ consciousness constitutes _this_ imaginary object with _this_ character of inexhaustible fullness. The reconstructed argument offers a transcendental answer. Sartre offers no equivalent explanation.

*Verdict:* _L'Imaginaire_ partially explains the cognitive dimension of aesthetic experience. It does not account for bodily constitution or the specific character of the inexhaustible noematic horizon. The reconstructed argument *survives this critique with qualifications.*

=== Test 2: Against the Esquisse

*Sartre's critique:* _Sehnsucht_ is an emotional response in which consciousness magically transforms the aesthetic situation to manage existential weight.

*The reconstructed argument's response:*

*First:* Sartre's account describes the _structure_ of the emotional act. It does not establish that the _noematic content_ of the emotional act is illusory. The fact that _Sehnsucht_ involves a magical reconfiguration of experience does not establish that what is disclosed through that reconfiguration is unreal. Husserl shows that perception involves complex intentional syntheses beyond what is strictly given — this does not make perception unreliable.

*Second:* The Steinian account of _Einfühlung_ provides a check on magical transformation. If _Sehnsucht_ were purely a private emotional-magical response, it would vary arbitrarily across subjects and cultures. But _Einfühlung_ reveals that the structure of aesthetic longing is intersubjectively shared in a way that arbitrary magical transformation cannot explain.

*Third:* Sartre's _Esquisse_ account is phenomenologically thin relative to the reconstruction. It operates at the level of the natural attitude rather than the reduced level the reconstruction requires.

*Verdict:* The _Esquisse_ critique identifies a genuine feature of _Sehnsucht_ — its emotional character involves an element of magical reconfiguration. But this does not establish that the experience is _merely_ magical transformation. The reconstructed argument *partially survives this critique.*

=== Test 3: Against L'Être et le Néant

==== Sub-test 3a: The Pour-soi and the Desire for God

*Sartre's critique:* The desire to be God is evidence of constitutive lack, not of God's existence. The desire is condemned to failure.

*The reconstructed argument's response:*

The Steinian reconstruction does not argue from desire to God's existence. It argues from the transcendental conditions of aesthetic experience to the operative presence of Being. This is a fundamentally different structure.

Sartre's critique is decisive against Lewis's original P3. It is *not* decisive against the transcendental argument.

The Steinian question cuts deeper: *what is the condition of possibility of _néantisation_ itself?*

_Néantisation_ requires:

* A consciousness capable of holding beings at distance.
* A field of Being within which this distancing occurs.
* An operative presence of Being that the _pour-soi_ implicitly relies on even in its acts of negation.

*The Steinian counter:* The _pour-soi_'s very capacity for _néantisation_ is a participation in Being — not in the massive, opaque Being of the _en-soi_, but in the inexhaustible fullness of _esse_ that grounds both _pour-soi_ and _en-soi_ without being reducible to either.

Sartre has no response to this within _L'Être et le Néant_ because he does not pursue the question of what grounds the _pour-soi/en-soi_ distinction itself. He treats it as ontologically ultimate. Stein treats it as ontologically derivative.

*Verdict:* This sub-test produces a genuine philosophical impasse. Sartre's ontology treats the _pour-soi/en-soi_ distinction as ultimate. Stein's ontology treats it as grounded in a deeper participation in Being. Neither position can be established without presupposing what it sets out to prove. *This is a genuine undecidable.*

==== Sub-test 3b: The Bad Faith Critique

*Sartre's critique:* Lewis's argument converts radical existential openness into metaphysical destiny — a constitutive _telos_ toward divine Beauty. This is the structure of bad faith.

*The reconstructed argument's response:*

*First:* The reconstructed argument does not claim that human persons have a fixed _telos_ toward God in the sense that bad faith involves. It claims that aesthetic experience discloses the operative presence of Being as a transcendental condition — a claim about the conditions of possible experience, not a claim about human destiny.

*Second:* Stein's account is not a denial of freedom. It is a claim about what freedom is _situated within_. The _pour-soi_ is radically free — but it is radically free _within_ a field of Being that it participates in and cannot nihilate. Freedom and participation in Being are not mutually exclusive.

*Third — honest residual tension:* The reconstructed argument does carry an implicit anthropological commitment: if Being is operatively present in aesthetic experience as its condition of possibility, then human subjectivity is not simply the radical nothingness of the _pour-soi_. It is a _situated_ freedom — freedom that participates in something it did not create and cannot negate. Sartre would reject this. The disagreement cannot be resolved by phenomenological analysis alone. It requires a choice between competing ontologies.

*Verdict:* The bad faith critique retains partial force. The reconstruction neutralizes its strongest form but cannot fully escape its implications. *Partial survival — with honest acknowledgment of residual tension.*

==== Sub-test 3c: The Intersubjectivity Confrontation

*Sartre's critique:* The _Regard_ (Look) structures intersubjectivity as mutual objectification. There is no shared intersubjective field — only competing subjectivities that objectify one another.

*The reconstructed argument's response:*

Sartre's account of intersubjectivity is structured by *conflict* — the Other's look reduces me to an object, I recover my freedom by looking back and reducing the Other. There is no genuine _with_ — only a perpetual oscillation between _pour-soi_ and _en-soi_ positions.

Stein's _Einfühlung_ offers a structurally different account:

* The Other's experience is apprehended _as theirs_ — not as a threat to my subjectivity but as a genuine other subjectivity that enriches the intersubjective field.
* The shared apprehension of the beautiful object is constituted through _Einfühlung_ — a genuine cognitive act, not a mutual objectification.

The Steinian argument holds the stronger phenomenological case: Sartre's _Regard_ is a _founded_ mode of intersubjectivity — it presupposes a more basic level of _Einfühlung_ that it then negates. The _Regard_ describes a specific mode of encounter (shame, pride, conflict). It does not account for the full range of intersubjective experience, including the pre-reflective bodily resonance that Merleau-Ponty also identifies as primary.

*Verdict:* Sartre's intersubjectivity account is a _founded_ mode that presupposes a more basic level of _Einfühlung_. The reconstructed argument *survives this critique.*

== Part IX: Ontological and Ethical Implications

=== Ontological Implications of Lewis's Original Premises

If Lewis's original argument succeeds, the following ontological commitments follow necessarily:

*O1.* The world is not ontologically self-sufficient. It is constituted as a sign-system pointing beyond itself.

*O2.* Human subjectivity has a constitutive _telos_ — an orientation toward transcendent Beauty — that precedes individual choice.

*O3.* Finite beauty is _participatory_ — it instantiates, however partially, a transcendent Form. This is crypto-Platonism with a Christian gloss.

*O4.* These commitments are not argued from neutral premises. They are assumed and then confirmed by the argument. The argument is structurally *circular*.

=== Ontological Implications of the Reconstructed Argument

The reconstruction shifts the ontological commitments significantly:

*O1 (Revised).* The world discloses an inexhaustible fullness of Being operative within finite experience — not as a separate realm inferred from the world but as the ground of its intelligibility.

*O2 (Revised).* Human subjectivity is _situated_ freedom — participating in Being as the condition of its own acts, including its acts of negation.

*O3 (Revised).* These commitments are established transcendentally, not assumed. They are grounded in the structure of possible experience.

=== Ethical Implications

*E1.* Aesthetic experience carries moral weight. Aesthetic insensitivity becomes a quasi-moral failing on Lewis's original account.

*E2.* The proper response to beauty is not possession but longing and worship — a specific ascetic-contemplative ethics is entailed.

*E3.* The human person is defined substantially by _desire_ — not by reason (contra Kant), not by facticity (contra Sartre), not by bodily being-in-the-world (contra Merleau-Ponty).

*E4 (Sartrean counter).* Defining human persons by a constitutive _telos_ toward God is bad faith — a denial of radical freedom dressed as metaphysical insight.

*E5 (Steinian counter).* The ethical response to aesthetic experience is _Offenheit_ — openness toward Being — not the premature closure of a theistic inference. This is more measured and more philosophically defensible than Lewis's original position.

=== What the Reconstruction Achieves

The Husserlian-Steinian reconstruction transforms Lewis's argument in four decisive ways:

*First:* It replaces the false Desire-Object Principle with a rigorous account of intentional horizon-structure and noematic inexhaustibility. The argument no longer depends on a metaphysical claim about desire-object correspondence.

*Second:* It grounds the universalization move in _Einfühlung_ — providing a rigorous intersubjective foundation for what Lewis merely asserted.

*Third:* It replaces the crude inference from datum to God with a transcendental argument — showing transcendence as the condition of the datum's possibility rather than its inferred conclusion.

*Fourth:* It honestly limits the conclusion: phenomenology discloses the operative presence of an inexhaustible fullness of Being. It does not by its own resources establish the Christian God.

=== What Does Not Survive

Two things must be stated honestly:

*First:* The reconstructed argument cannot establish the Christian God by phenomenological means alone. Stein herself acknowledges this. The specifically theistic conclusion requires faith as a distinct mode of intentional access — not as an irrational supplement but as a genuinely different intentional relation to the Being that phenomenology discloses obliquely.

*Second:* The confrontation between Stein's participatory ontology and Sartre's radical ontology of the _pour-soi_ produces a genuine undecidable. The choice between them is not a phenomenological choice — it is an ontological one, and ultimately an existential one.

=== The Argument's Genuine Contribution

Lewis correctly insists that aesthetic experience is *philosophically informative* — that it cannot be exhausted by neurological or cultural-constructivist accounts without remainder. This is a serious and defensible position shared by Ingarden, Gadamer, and Ricoeur.

Stein shows that this instinct can be developed with genuine phenomenological rigor. The failure is not in Lewis's intuition. The failure is threefold:

* The *inferential structure* is too crude for the weight it must bear.
* The *phenomenological description* of _Sehnsucht_ is pre-critical.
* The *move to theism* requires philosophical resources Lewis does not deploy and cannot generate from within his argument alone.

=== Final Judgment

*On Lewis's original argument:* Phenomenologically evocative, logically underdetermined, intersubjectively naive, and ontologically circular. Its rhetorical and pastoral power is real. Its claim to philosophical sufficiency does not survive contact with the tradition it implicitly invokes.

*On the reconstructed argument:* A philosophically serious argument that survives Sartre's technical critiques in _L'Imaginaire_ and _Esquisse_, survives the desire-for-God critique by operating at the transcendental rather than the motivational level, partially survives the bad faith critique while acknowledging residual tension, and decisively survives the intersubjectivity critique through _Einfühlung_.

*On the ultimate question:* The Husserlian-Steinian reconstruction demonstrates that aesthetic experience, phenomenologically analyzed with full rigor, discloses the operative presence of an inexhaustible fullness of Being that cannot be fully accounted for within a purely immanentist framework — and that this disclosure is intersubjectively grounded, bodily constituted, and transcendentally necessary.

Whether that disclosure points toward the Christian God remains, on strictly phenomenological grounds, an open question.

What is no longer open is whether the question itself is philosophically serious.

*It is.*
